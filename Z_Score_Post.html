Z-score

Where am I on the normal distribution?

What is a Z-score?

Z-score is a number that signifies how far a raw number is from the mean by a number of standard deviations. Wait, what do you mean by a raw number? Raw numbers are just the values that you originally have to analyze like scores on a test, but they become z-scores after a mathematical transformation. It is also important to note that this transformation is valid if the distribution of the raw values takes on the form of a normal distribution. After the transformation, they are essentially "cooked" and ready to be "served," i.e., interpreted. Let's say the mean score is 70 out of 100 with a standard deviation of 20, and we have other scores like 50 and 95. The equation from the featured image is used, the mean would then be transformed to 0 because it is at the mean; lower values than the mean would be negative with 50 becoming -1; and, higher values than the mean would be positive with 95 becoming +1.25.

In the illustration above, the bell curves have vertical dashed lines with the center being the mean and others to the left and right in increments of 1 standard deviation--with 3 on the left and 3 on the right. The values labeled on the top are the raw values of the scores, and the bottom we have -1 standard deviation and +1 standard deviation to the left and right respectively. The standard deviations continue as -2 and -3 on the left and +2 and +3 on the right, but are left out to not crowd the graph. I have labeled the raw values in black that transform into the z-scores shown in blue. The vertical line in blue shows its final calculated location showing its relative position.

By using a Z-score, it becomes easier to compare where any particular raw number is in comparison to the rest of the numbers because it is based on a common reference point: the mean. We know the direction the relative value is by the sign of the z-score, and we know roughly how far the value is from the mean by the magnitude of the number. Moreover, knowing where a value relative to others can be done by percentile because the area under the distribution sums up to 1, meaning parts of the curve are essentially fractions of 100, and a raw value can be 0.75, the 75 percentile, being a value 75% above all other values in relation to the set of values. More of this will be clarified with worked out examples below.

What is a Z-table?

However, before more calculations, it is best to know your tools. One of them is the Z-table also known as the standard normal table. This can actually be shown as two tables, but the idea is that the z-score corresponds to the cumulative amount of area it covers from the left most end of the normal distribution towards the right to its exact z-score. One table shows z-scores that are negative, so the z-scores are to the left of the mean. Inversely, the other table shows positive z-scores that are to the right of the mean. The rows on the left of the table are one's and tenth's digit of the z-score, and the columns above show the hundreth's digit of the z-score. The intersection of the row and column is just the score added together. For example, if one had a z-score of -2.35, the row would be -2.3 and the column would be 0.05, so the combination would be -2.35. With that in mind, the intersection is the area that is covered from the far left of the distribution to that exact z-score. Because the total area of the population sums to 1, this area corresponds to the probability of having those range of values, or the percentile score.
